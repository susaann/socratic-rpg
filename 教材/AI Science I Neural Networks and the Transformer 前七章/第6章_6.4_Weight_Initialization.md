## 6.4 Weight Initialization

Initialization appears to be a minor implementation detail. It is not. Incorrect initialization causes training to fail from the very first step.

### Why Not Zero Initialization?

Setting all weights to zero W(l)=0\mathbf{W}^{(l)} = \mathbf{0}W(l)=0:

  * All neurons in each layer receive identical input, compute identical pre-activations, and produce identical outputs
  * All neurons receive identical gradients in backpropagation, and receive identical updates
  * After any number of steps, all neurons in each layer remain identical



This **symmetry never breaks**. The network behaves as if it had width 1 at every hidden layer, regardless of its nominal architecture. The fix: initialize weights with different values drawn from a random distribution.

Bias initialization to zero is safe: the randomness in the weight matrices breaks the symmetry between neurons. Biases are scalars that shift individual neurons; they do not mediate inter-neuron symmetry.

### Variance Propagation Analysis

The key question: what distribution should weights be drawn from? Too large and activations explode; too small and activations vanish.

**Linear analysis** : for a layer with dl−1d_{l-1}dl−1​ inputs, assuming Wji(l)W_{ji}^{(l)}Wji(l)​ are i.i.d. with E[Wji]=0\mathbb{E}[W_{ji}] = 0E[Wji​]=0 and Var(Wji)=σw2\text{Var}(W_{ji}) = \sigma_w^2Var(Wji​)=σw2​, and inputs hi(l−1)h_i^{(l-1)}hi(l−1)​ are i.i.d. with variance vl−1v_{l-1}vl−1​:

Var(zj(l))=dl−1⋅σw2⋅vl−1\text{Var}(z_j^{(l)}) = d_{l-1} \cdot \sigma_w^2 \cdot v_{l-1}Var(zj(l)​)=dl−1​⋅σw2​⋅vl−1​

After LLL layers: vL=(dl−1σw2)Lv0v_L = (d_{l-1} \sigma_w^2)^L v_0vL​=(dl−1​σw2​)Lv0​.

  * If dl−1σw2>1d_{l-1}\sigma_w^2 > 1dl−1​σw2​>1: variance explodes exponentially
  * If dl−1σw2<1d_{l-1}\sigma_w^2 < 1dl−1​σw2​<1: variance vanishes exponentially
  * If dl−1σw2=1d_{l-1}\sigma_w^2 = 1dl−1​σw2​=1: variance is stable — this is the target



The same analysis applies to gradients in backpropagation: unstable variance in the forward pass implies unstable gradient magnitude in the backward pass.

### Xavier / Glorot Initialization (for sigmoid and tanh)

**Goal** (Glorot & Bengio, 2010): maintain stable variance in both forward and backward passes.

**Forward pass requirement** : dl−1σw2=1 ⟹ σw2=1/dl−1d_{l-1} \sigma_w^2 = 1 \implies \sigma_w^2 = 1/d_{l-1}dl−1​σw2​=1⟹σw2​=1/dl−1​ (fan-in condition)

**Backward pass requirement** : dlσw2=1 ⟹ σw2=1/dld_l \sigma_w^2 = 1 \implies \sigma_w^2 = 1/d_ldl​σw2​=1⟹σw2​=1/dl​ (fan-out condition)

Since both requirements cannot be satisfied simultaneously in general, Glorot and Bengio take the harmonic mean:

σw2=2dl−1+dl\boxed{\sigma_w^2 = \frac{2}{d_{l-1} + d_l}}σw2​=dl−1​+dl​2​​

Implementations:

  * **Uniform** : W∼Uniform ⁣(−6dl−1+dl, +6dl−1+dl)W \sim \text{Uniform}\\!\left(-\sqrt{\tfrac{6}{d_{l-1}+d_l}},\; +\sqrt{\tfrac{6}{d_{l-1}+d_l}}\right)W∼Uniform(−dl−1​+dl​6​​,+dl−1​+dl​6​​) (variance of Uniform(−a,a)\text{Uniform}(-a,a)Uniform(−a,a) is a2/3a^2/3a2/3, set equal to σw2\sigma_w^2σw2​ and solve for aaa)
  * **Normal** : W∼N ⁣(0,2dl−1+dl)W \sim \mathcal{N}\\!\left(0, \tfrac{2}{d_{l-1}+d_l}\right)W∼N(0,dl−1​+dl​2​)



**Appropriate for** : sigmoid and tanh (the analysis assumes the activation is approximately linear near zero, which holds for tanh but approximately for sigmoid).

### He / Kaiming Initialization (for ReLU)

Xavier's derivation assumed the activation is linear near zero. ReLU violates this: it zeroes out the negative half, halving the variance passing through each layer.

**ReLU variance analysis** : if z∼N(0,σz2)z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_z^2)z∼N(0,σz2​), then h=ReLU(z)h = \text{ReLU}(z)h=ReLU(z):

E[ReLU(z)2]=12E[z2]=σz22\mathbb{E}[\text{ReLU}(z)^2] = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}[z^2] = \frac{\sigma_z^2}{2}E[ReLU(z)2]=21​E[z2]=2σz2​​

(exactly half the values are zeroed, reducing the second moment by half).

So the forward pass variance propagation becomes:

Var(h(l))=dl−1⋅σw2⋅12⋅Var(h(l−1))\text{Var}(h^{(l)}) = d_{l-1} \cdot \sigma_w^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{Var}(h^{(l-1)})Var(h(l))=dl−1​⋅σw2​⋅21​⋅Var(h(l−1))

Setting this equal to Var(h(l−1))\text{Var}(h^{(l-1)})Var(h(l−1)) for stability:

dl−1⋅σw2⋅12=1 ⟹ σw2=2dl−1d_{l-1} \cdot \sigma_w^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 1 \implies \sigma_w^2 = \frac{2}{d_{l-1}}dl−1​⋅σw2​⋅21​=1⟹σw2​=dl−1​2​ He init: W∼N ⁣(0, 2dl−1)\boxed{\text{He init: } W \sim \mathcal{N}\\!\left(0,\; \frac{2}{d_{l-1}}\right)}He init: W∼N(0,dl−1​2​)​

The factor 2 in the numerator (vs. the 1 in fan-in Xavier) compensates for ReLU's half-zeroing effect.

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection: Signal Preservation in Electrical Engineering, Renormalization Groups, and Stochastic Processes**
> 
> **Electrical engineering** faces the same variance preservation problem in filter design. A cascade of LLL amplifiers, each with gain ggg, produces total gain gLg^LgL. For ∣g∣<1|g| < 1∣g∣<1 the signal decays to zero; for ∣g∣>1|g| > 1∣g∣>1 it saturates or clips. Impedance matching in analog circuits is the principle of equating source and load impedance to maximize power transfer — an analogous variance-preservation condition.
> 
> **The renormalization group** (Wilson, 1971) in statistical physics studies how physical systems behave at different length scales. Renormalization group flows track how coupling constants change under scale transformations. Fixed points of the renormalization group — where constants don't change with scale — are exactly analogous to the variance-stable initialization conditions: the condition dl−1σw2=1d_{l-1}\sigma_w^2 = 1dl−1​σw2​=1 is a fixed point of the layer-by-layer variance propagation map.
> 
> **Stochastic processes** : a random walk Xt=X0+∑k=1tξkX_t = X_0 + \sum_{k=1}^t \xi_kXt​=X0​+∑k=1t​ξk​ has variance t⋅Var(ξ)t \cdot \text{Var}(\xi)t⋅Var(ξ), growing linearly with depth. The variance-preservation conditions for neural networks are precisely the conditions under which the variance of the forward-pass activations does not grow with depth — they are the conditions for a mean-zero martingale rather than a random walk.
