### Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

Each step uses a **single randomly chosen example** to estimate the gradient:

‚ąáwL(w)‚Čą‚ąáw‚Ąď(f(xj;w),yj),j‚ąľUniform{1,‚Ä¶,n}\nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}) \approx \nabla_\mathbf{w} \ell(f(\mathbf{x}_j; \mathbf{w}), y_j), \quad j \sim \text{Uniform}\\{1, \ldots, n\\}‚ąáw‚ÄčL(w)‚Čą‚ąáw‚Äč‚Ąď(f(xj‚Äč;w),yj‚Äč),j‚ąľUniform{1,‚Ä¶,n}

**Key property ‚ÄĒ unbiasedness** : the single-example gradient is an unbiased estimator of the full gradient:

This follows directly from the definition of expectation over a uniform draw: each index jjj is selected with probability 1/n1/n1/n, so the expected value of the single-sample gradient is the average over all samples.

The gradient estimate has noise (variance O(1)O(1)O(1)) but the correct expected direction. This noise turns out to have three beneficial effects:

  1. **Escape from saddle points and local minima** : random perturbations occasionally move parameters toward better regions of the loss surface
  2. **Implicit regularization** : the noise of SGD biases convergence toward _flat_ minima ‚ÄĒ regions where the loss changes slowly with parameters. Flat minima generalize better because the model is insensitive to small distribution shifts
  3. **Computational efficiency** : each update costs O(p)O(p)O(p) rather than O(np)O(np)O(np)
