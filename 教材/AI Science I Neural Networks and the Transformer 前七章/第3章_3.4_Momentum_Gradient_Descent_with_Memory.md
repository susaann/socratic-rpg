## 3.4 Momentum: Gradient Descent with Memory

### The Ravine Problem

Pure SGD performs poorly on loss surfaces with "ravines" — regions where the surface is steep in one direction and shallow in another. In a ravine, the gradient in the steep direction is large but alternates sign at each step (oscillation), while the gradient in the shallow direction is small and consistent. The result: parameters zig-zag across the ravine while advancing slowly along it.

### Momentum: Physics and Mathematics

**Physical analogy** : imagine a ball rolling on the loss surface. Without momentum, the ball responds only to the current slope — it has no inertia and forgets its previous trajectory. With momentum, the ball has mass: it accelerates in consistent downhill directions and decelerates when the slope reverses.

### Why Momentum Fixes the Ravine Problem

Consider a loss surface with two directions:

  * **Oscillating direction** (across the ravine): gradient alternates sign — +g⊥,−g⊥,+g⊥,…+g_\perp, -g_\perp, +g_\perp, \ldots+g⊥​,−g⊥​,+g⊥​,…
  * **Consistent direction** (along the ravine): gradient has fixed sign — g∥,g∥,g∥,…g_\parallel, g_\parallel, g_\parallel, \ldotsg∥​,g∥​,g∥​,…



**In the oscillating direction** : the EWMA accumulates positive and negative contributions that cancel:

v⊥≈ηg⊥(1−γ+γ2−⋯ )≈ηg⊥1+γv_\perp \approx \eta g_\perp \left(1 - \gamma + \gamma^2 - \cdots\right) \approx \frac{\eta g_\perp}{1+\gamma}v⊥​≈ηg⊥​(1−γ+γ2−⋯)≈1+γηg⊥​​

The speed is suppressed by a factor of (1+γ)−1(1+\gamma)^{-1}(1+γ)−1 compared to pure SGD.

**In the consistent direction** : all contributions have the same sign and accumulate geometrically:

v∥≈ηg∥(1+γ+γ2+⋯ )=ηg∥1−γv_\parallel \approx \eta g_\parallel (1 + \gamma + \gamma^2 + \cdots) = \frac{\eta g_\parallel}{1-\gamma}v∥​≈ηg∥​(1+γ+γ2+⋯)=1−γηg∥​​

The speed is **amplified** by a factor of (1−γ)−1(1-\gamma)^{-1}(1−γ)−1. For γ=0.9\gamma = 0.9γ=0.9, this is a 10× amplification.

Momentum simultaneously damps oscillations and accelerates consistent progress — exactly the behavior needed to navigate ravines efficiently.

### Nesterov Momentum

Nesterov (1983) proposed a modification: compute the gradient at the **predicted next position** rather than the current position:

vt+1=γvt+η∇wL(wt−γvt)\mathbf{v}_{t+1} = \gamma \mathbf{v}_t + \eta \nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_t - \gamma \mathbf{v}_t)vt+1​=γvt​+η∇w​L(wt​−γvt​) wt+1=wt−vt+1\mathbf{w}_{t+1} = \mathbf{w}_t - \mathbf{v}_{t+1}wt+1​=wt​−vt+1​

The gradient is evaluated at wt−γvt\mathbf{w}_t - \gamma \mathbf{v}_twt​−γvt​ — where momentum alone would take the parameters. This "lookahead" improves the convergence rate on convex objectives from O(1/T)O(1/T)O(1/T) to O(1/T2)O(1/T^2)O(1/T2).

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection: EWMA in Signal Processing, Finance, and Atmospheric Science**
> 
> **Signal processing** uses the exponentially weighted moving average as a first-order infinite impulse response (IIR) low-pass filter: yt=αxt+(1−α)yt−1y_t = \alpha x_t + (1-\alpha) y_{t-1}yt​=αxt​+(1−α)yt−1​. The parameter α=1−γ\alpha = 1 - \gammaα=1−γ controls the cutoff frequency. Momentum optimization is a gradient low-pass filter — it passes the slowly changing "signal" component of the gradient (consistent direction) and attenuates the rapidly oscillating "noise" (ravine oscillations).
> 
> **Finance** uses EWMA to estimate time-varying volatility: the RiskMetrics model (J.P. Morgan, 1994) estimates variance as σt2=λσt−12+(1−λ)εt−12\sigma_t^2 = \lambda \sigma_{t-1}^2 + (1-\lambda)\varepsilon_{t-1}^2σt2​=λσt−12​+(1−λ)εt−12​. The decay parameter λ\lambdaλ corresponds directly to the momentum coefficient γ\gammaγ, and the mathematical structure is identical. Engle's GARCH(1,1) model is the same EWMA applied to conditional variance estimation, work for which he received the 2003 Nobel Prize in Economics.
> 
> **Atmospheric science** uses EWMA for recursive estimation in weather forecasting. The Kalman filter — the optimal linear state estimator under Gaussian noise — reduces to EWMA when the noise covariance is stationary. Momentum's gradient-smoothing operation is a degenerate Kalman filter that treats the "true gradient" as a hidden state to be estimated from noisy observations.
