## 3.7 Chapter Summary

Gradient descent is necessary for deep learning because neural network loss surfaces are non-convex (no closed-form solution exists) and high-dimensional (exact second-order methods are computationally infeasible). The three variants — batch, stochastic, and mini-batch — trade gradient accuracy for computational efficiency. SGD's noise is not merely a nuisance but actively helps generalization via implicit regularization toward flat minima.

Learning rate is the most consequential hyperparameter. Schedules (step decay, cosine annealing, warmup) adapt the learning rate to the stage of training. Momentum adds gradient memory as an EWMA, simultaneously suppressing oscillations and amplifying consistent directions. Adam combines momentum with per-parameter adaptive scaling and bias correction, making it robust across a wide range of architectures and hyperparameter settings.

Non-convex optimization succeeds in practice because: high-dimensional saddle points are overwhelmingly more common than local minima, all local minima in over-parameterized networks are nearly equivalent in loss value, and SGD's implicit bias selects for well-generalizing flat minima.
