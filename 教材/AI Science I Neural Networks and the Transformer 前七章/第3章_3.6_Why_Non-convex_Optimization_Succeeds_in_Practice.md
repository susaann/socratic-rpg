## 3.6 Why Non-convex Optimization Succeeds in Practice

Neural network loss surfaces are non-convex. Gradient descent has no theoretical guarantee of finding the global minimum. Yet deep learning works. Several phenomena explain this gap between theory and practice.

**High-dimensional saddle points dominate** : for a function in Rp\mathbb{R}^pRp, a local minimum requires all ppp eigenvalues of the Hessian to be positive. Under random matrix models, this probability decreases exponentially with ppp. Dauphin et al. (2014) showed that in high-dimensional loss surfaces, most zero-gradient points are saddle points rather than local minima. SGD's noise naturally escapes saddle points — there always exists a downhill direction, and random fluctuations will eventually find it.

**Most local minima are equally good** : Choromanska et al. (2015) connected deep network loss surfaces to spin glass models in statistical physics. Their analysis suggests that for sufficiently large networks, the loss values of different local minima are concentrated near the global minimum — all good solutions are roughly as good as each other.

**Over-parameterization helps, not hurts** : when the number of parameters ppp greatly exceeds the number of training examples nnn, the loss surface develops many paths to zero training loss (infinitely many solutions exist). SGD selects among these solutions, gravitating toward a specific type: the solution with minimum norm, or equivalently, the flattest minimum. This is called the **implicit bias of gradient descent**. Flat minima generalize well because small distributional shifts do not change the loss value significantly.

**The double descent phenomenon** : increasing model capacity beyond the interpolation threshold (where training loss reaches zero) does not necessarily increase test error — it often decreases it. This "double descent" behavior contradicts the classical bias-variance expectation but is consistently observed in neural networks. Theoretical explanations involve kernel methods, benign overfitting, and the implicit regularization of gradient descent.
