### Application Problems

**3.5** You are training a model and observe the following: for the first 20 epochs, training loss decreases smoothly. Then, at epoch 21, training loss suddenly jumps up by 50%. Using your knowledge of learning rate schedules, diagnose the most likely cause and explain the fix.

Answer

**Diagnosis** : the jump at epoch 21 is almost certainly caused by a **step-decay learning rate schedule** that reduces the learning rate too aggressively _before_ it should, or more likely in the opposite direction ‚Äî a step that **increases** the learning rate abruptly, or (more commonly in buggy implementations) an incorrect learning rate that was mistakenly configured at an intermediate epoch. However the more common cause of a sudden _jump up_ in training loss at a scheduled step is: the learning rate was _already too large_ and a schedule that should have decayed it instead was misconfigured, or warmup ends and the full learning rate is reached before the model is ready.

**More likely scenario given the magnitude (50% jump)** : the learning rate at epoch 21 is far too large ‚Äî the model is in a well-optimized region (smooth decrease through epoch 20), and a large learning rate step overshoots the minimum. This can happen if a warmup schedule ends at epoch 20 and the "post-warmup" learning rate is misconfigured to be too high.

**Fix** :

  1. Inspect the learning rate schedule ‚Äî plot Œ∑t\eta_tŒ∑t‚Äã vs epoch to verify what happens at epoch 21
  2. If the learning rate jumps up, fix the schedule (warmup should increase to a sensible peak, not beyond it)
  3. If the loss surface is genuinely sensitive to this learning rate, use cosine annealing rather than step decay to avoid abrupt changes
  4. Reduce the peak learning rate ‚Äî if the model was learning well at epoch 20, the appropriate learning rate is close to what was used at epoch 20, not higher

### Think Deeper

**3.7** The claim that SGD has "implicit regularization" toward flat minima is widely cited. Describe what a flat minimum is mathematically, why it should generalize better than a sharp minimum, and what evidence (theoretical or empirical) supports this claim. Where does this argument break down?

Answer

**Definition of flat minimum** : a local minimum w‚àó\mathbf{w}^*w‚àó is flat if the Hessian H=‚àá2L(w‚àó)\mathbf{H} = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}^*)H=‚àá2L(w‚àó) has small eigenvalues. Geometrically, a flat minimum occupies a broad basin in the loss surface ‚Äî small perturbations to w‚àó\mathbf{w}^*w‚àó lead to small changes in the loss. A sharp minimum has a Hessian with large eigenvalues ‚Äî the loss rises steeply in many directions when the parameters are perturbed.

**Why flat minima should generalize better** : test data is drawn from the same distribution as training data but is not identical. The distribution shift corresponds to a perturbation in the effective loss landscape. If the minimum is flat, this perturbation changes the loss only slightly ‚Äî the model performs similarly on training and test data. If the minimum is sharp, the same perturbation can cause a large increase in loss. Formally, the sharpness-generalization connection is supported by PAC-Bayes bounds (Langford & Caruana, 2002): the generalization gap is bounded by a term proportional to the sharpness of the minimum (measured by the trace or largest eigenvalue of the Hessian).

**Evidence** :

  * _Empirical_ : Keskar et al. (2017) showed that large mini-batches converge to sharper minima and exhibit worse test performance. Small mini-batches (more noise) converge to flatter minima and generalize better.
  * _Theoretical_ : the implicit bias of gradient descent in the linear case favors minimum-norm solutions (Soudry et al., 2018). For neural networks, the analysis is less complete, but over-parameterization theory suggests SGD selects "max-margin" or "minimum-norm interpolating" solutions.



**Where it breaks down** :

  * The connection is not always causal: some sharp minima generalize well, and some flat minima overfit. The metric of "flatness" is not invariant to reparameterization of the network (multiplying one layer by a constant ccc and dividing the next by ccc gives the same function but a different curvature).
  * Very large batch training with carefully tuned learning rate schedules (LARS, LAMB) can achieve flat minima despite reduced noise.
  * The phenomenon of double descent suggests that generalization is not simply monotone in flatness.



The flat-minima story is a useful intuition but not a complete theory.
