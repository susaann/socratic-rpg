## 5.7 Chapter Summary

Backpropagation is the chain rule applied systematically to a computational graph. Every neural network operation (linear transform, activation, loss) has a known local gradient. The backward pass traverses the graph in reverse topological order, accumulating gradients by multiplying the upstream gradient by the local gradient at each node.

The delta formalism captures the key recursion: δ(l)=[(W(l+1))⊤δ(l+1)]⊙σ′(l)(z(l))\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)} = [(\mathbf{W}^{(l+1)})^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l+1)}] \odot \sigma'^{(l)}(\mathbf{z}^{(l)})δ(l)=[(W(l+1))⊤δ(l+1)]⊙σ′(l)(z(l)). The two universal parameter gradient formulas are ∂L/∂W(l)=δ(l)(h(l−1))⊤\partial L/\partial \mathbf{W}^{(l)} = \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)} (\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)})^\top∂L/∂W(l)=δ(l)(h(l−1))⊤ and ∂L/∂b(l)=δ(l)\partial L/\partial \mathbf{b}^{(l)} = \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)}∂L/∂b(l)=δ(l). These formulas apply to any depth, any width, and any differentiable activation function.

The Dead ReLU problem is gradient blocking in action: a neuron with negative pre-activation has zero derivative, which stops all gradient flow through that neuron. The Leaky ReLU and GELU activations from Chapter 4 exist precisely to solve this problem.

Backpropagation costs the same as one forward pass, making it vastly more efficient than finite-difference gradient approximation. This computational efficiency — combined with GPU parallelism for mini-batches — is what makes training networks with millions of parameters tractable. The combination of backpropagation (Chapter 5) and gradient descent (Chapter 3) is the complete training algorithm for everything in this volume.
