## 1.3 The Paradigm Shift: From Writing Rules to Learning from Data

The three paradigms differ in _where_ the intelligence lives. In symbolic AI, it lives in the rules — written by humans and executed by machines. In connectionism, it lives in the weights — numerical parameters adjusted automatically by gradient descent. In statistical learning, it lives in the statistical structure of the data — captured by a learned model with theoretical guarantees.

The practical shift from the first paradigm to the second and third is best understood through examples.

**Example: recognizing objects in images.** The traditional approach (dominant until ~2012) required computer vision experts to manually design _feature extractors_ — algorithms that computed specific numerical summaries of image patches (edge orientations, color histograms, texture statistics), which were then fed to a classifier. Designing a good feature extractor required years of domain expertise and worked only for the specific domain it was designed for. A feature extractor designed for face recognition was useless for plant identification. The deep learning approach: train a convolutional network directly on raw pixels, letting it discover its own feature hierarchy. The network learns edge detectors in early layers, part detectors in middle layers, and object detectors in later layers — without any explicit instruction about what to look for.

**Example: understanding language.** The traditional approach required linguists to build grammars, parsers, named entity recognizers, and coreference resolution systems — each a specialized component requiring deep linguistic expertise, trained separately, and assembled into a pipeline. The current approach: train a single Transformer on a massive text corpus to predict the next token, then fine-tune it on the task of interest. The same pretrained model achieves state-of-the-art performance on translation, summarization, question answering, and reasoning — without any task-specific architectural design.

The pattern is consistent. In every domain where it has been applied with sufficient data and computation, the "learn from data" approach has displaced hand-crafted feature engineering, not because hand-crafted features are wrong but because learned features are better.

This shift can be summarized in a sequence:

Symbolic AI:human writes rules→machine executes\text{Symbolic AI}: \quad \text{human writes rules} \to \text{machine executes}Symbolic AI:human writes rules→machine executes Statistical ML:human designs features+machine learns rule\text{Statistical ML}: \quad \text{human designs features} + \text{machine learns rule}Statistical ML:human designs features+machine learns rule Deep Learning:machine learns features and rules from raw data\text{Deep Learning}: \quad \text{machine learns features and rules from raw data}Deep Learning:machine learns features and rules from raw data LLMs:machine learns general representations from massive unlabeled corpora\text{LLMs}: \quad \text{machine learns general representations from massive unlabeled corpora}LLMs:machine learns general representations from massive unlabeled corpora

Each step moves more of the cognitive work from human designer to machine learner, enabled by more data, more computation, and more effective training algorithms.
