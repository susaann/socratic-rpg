## 7.2 Batch Normalization

### 7.2.1 The Internal Covariate Shift Problem

As training proceeds, the weights in every layer change simultaneously. This means the distribution of inputs to each layer shifts continuously — a phenomenon Ioffe and Szegedy (2015) called **internal covariate shift**.

The consequence is severe: later layers must constantly adapt to changing input statistics, not just to the task. This forces smaller learning rates and careful initialization, slowing training substantially.

The intuition for the fix is simple: if we could force each layer's activations to have zero mean and unit variance at all times, internal covariate shift would vanish by construction.

### 7.2.2 The Batch Normalization Transform

Given a mini-batch of mmm samples {z1,z2,…,zm}\\{z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_m\\}{z1​,z2​,…,zm​} at a single neuron (or feature channel), Batch Normalization computes:

**Step 1: Compute mini-batch statistics.**

μB=1m∑i=1mziσB2=1m∑i=1m(zi−μB)2\mu_\mathcal{B} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m z_i \qquad \sigma^2_\mathcal{B} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m (z_i - \mu_\mathcal{B})^2μB​=m1​i=1∑m​zi​σB2​=m1​i=1∑m​(zi​−μB​)2

**Step 2: Normalize.**

z^i=zi−μBσB2+ϵ\hat{z}_i = \frac{z_i - \mu_\mathcal{B}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_\mathcal{B} + \epsilon}}z^i​=σB2​+ϵ​zi​−μB​​

where ϵ>0\epsilon > 0ϵ>0 (typically 10−510^{-5}10−5) prevents division by zero.

**Step 3: Scale and shift.**

yi=γz^i+βy_i = \gamma \hat{z}_i + \betayi​=γz^i​+β

Here γ\gammaγ and β\betaβ are **learnable parameters** — one pair per neuron/channel. This is a critical design decision: if the network finds it useful to have non-zero mean or non-unit variance, it can learn to recover any distribution via γ\gammaγ and β\betaβ.

The normalized values z^i\hat{z}_iz^i​ are what propagate into the activation function. The full BN transform is applied before the activation:

h(l)=σ ⁣(BN(W(l)h(l−1)+b(l)))\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \sigma\\!\left(\text{BN}(\mathbf{W}^{(l)} \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(l)})\right)h(l)=σ(BN(W(l)h(l−1)+b(l)))

Note: when using BN, the bias b(l)\mathbf{b}^{(l)}b(l) is redundant — the normalization step subtracts the mean, and β\betaβ absorbs the bias term. In practice, b(l)\mathbf{b}^{(l)}b(l) is omitted when BN is present.

Despite its complexity, the key insight is that the normalization statistics couple all examples in a mini-batch. Gradients now flow through the batch statistics, not just through individual neurons.

### 7.2.4 Training Mode vs. Inference Mode

A subtlety arises at inference time: we typically process one example at a time (or a small batch), so mini-batch statistics are unreliable or unavailable.

**Training mode** : Use mini-batch statistics μB\mu_\mathcal{B}μB​ and σB2\sigma^2_\mathcal{B}σB2​ computed on the current batch.

**Inference mode** : Use **running statistics** accumulated over training.

During training, BN maintains exponential moving averages:

μ^run←(1−α)μ^run+α μB\hat{\mu}_{\text{run}} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\hat{\mu}_{\text{run}} + \alpha\,\mu_\mathcal{B}μ^​run​←(1−α)μ^​run​+αμB​ σ^run2←(1−α)σ^run2+α σB2\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{run}} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{run}} + \alpha\,\sigma^2_\mathcal{B}σ^run2​←(1−α)σ^run2​+ασB2​

where α≈0.1\alpha \approx 0.1α≈0.1 is the momentum for the running statistics (not to be confused with optimizer momentum).

At inference time, normalization uses the frozen running statistics:

z^=z−μ^runσ^run2+ϵ\hat{z} = \frac{z - \hat{\mu}_{\text{run}}}{\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{run}} + \epsilon}}z^=σ^run2​+ϵ​z−μ^​run​​

This means a BN layer has two distinct operational modes. Failing to switch to inference mode (i.e., leaving the network in training mode during evaluation) causes inputs to be normalized against the current-batch statistics — a common bug that causes test performance to differ from development performance.

### 7.2.5 Why Batch Normalization Works: Three Mechanisms

**Mechanism 1: Direct gradient normalization.** Because activations are normalized, the Jacobian matrices Jk\mathbf{J}_kJk​ in the spectral radius product tend to have eigenvalues near 1. This keeps gradients from vanishing or exploding.

**Mechanism 2: Faster optimization.** Normalized activations create a loss landscape with more uniform curvature. Large learning rates are now safe — the network is less likely to explode out of a basin. BN typically enables 2–10× larger learning rates.

**Mechanism 3: Implicit regularization.** The batch statistics μB\mu_\mathcal{B}μB​ and σB2\sigma^2_\mathcal{B}σB2​ vary across mini-batches due to sampling randomness. This introduces noise into the forward pass — similar in effect to Dropout. BN-trained networks generalize better than equivalent networks trained without it, even holding everything else equal.

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection**
> 
> **Whitening in signal processing, statistics, and physics** : Batch Normalization is a restricted form of **whitening** — transforming data to have zero mean and unit covariance. Full whitening (also decorrelating features) is the foundation of **Principal Component Analysis** in statistics, **ZCA whitening** in image preprocessing, and **matched filtering** in signal processing. In **statistical physics** , the analogous operation is the transformation to reduced coordinates in thermodynamics: expressing energy in units of kBTk_BTkB​T makes dimensionless comparisons possible across scales. The "scale and shift" step (γ,β\gamma, \betaγ,β) is the key addition — it allows the network to learn which distributions are actually useful, rather than enforcing standardization unconditionally.

**Beyond Batch Normalization: Other Normalization Schemes.** Batch Normalization computes statistics over the batch dimension, which creates two limitations: (1) small batch sizes produce noisy estimates of μB\mu_\mathcal{B}μB​ and σB2\sigma^2_\mathcal{B}σB2​, degrading performance; (2) for variable-length sequences (Part III), different examples in a batch have different lengths, making batch statistics ill-defined. **Layer Normalization** (Ba et al., 2016) solves both problems by normalizing over the feature dimension for each example independently — no batch dependence at all. **Group Normalization** (Wu & He, 2018) normalizes over groups of channels, interpolating between Layer Norm (one group) and Instance Norm (one channel per group). **Instance Normalization** (Ulyanov et al., 2016) normalizes each channel separately and is standard in style transfer. In Chapter 22, Layer Normalization will replace Batch Normalization in the Transformer architecture — precisely because the Transformer processes variable-length sequences where batch statistics are unreliable.
