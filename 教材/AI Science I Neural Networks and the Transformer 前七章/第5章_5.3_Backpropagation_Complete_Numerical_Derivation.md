### Step 4: Propagate Gradient Through ReLU

We now need ∂L∂z(1)\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{z}^{(1)}}∂z(1)∂L​, passing through the ReLU activation.

The ReLU derivative is 1[z>0]\mathbb{1}[z > 0]1[z>0], a binary gate:

∂L∂zj(1)=∂L∂hj(1)⋅ReLU′(zj(1))=∂L∂hj(1)⋅1[zj(1)>0]\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_j^{(1)}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial h_j^{(1)}} \cdot \text{ReLU}'(z_j^{(1)}) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial h_j^{(1)}} \cdot \mathbb{1}[z_j^{(1)} > 0]∂zj(1)​∂L​=∂hj(1)​∂L​⋅ReLU′(zj(1)​)=∂hj(1)​∂L​⋅1[zj(1)​>0]

**Dead ReLU in action** : Neuron 1 had z1(1)=−0.7<0z_1^{(1)} = -0.7 < 0z1(1)​=−0.7<0. ReLU zeroed its forward output and also zeroes its backward gradient. This neuron contributes nothing to the forward computation and receives no gradient from the backward pass. Any weights that feed into neuron 1 will have zero gradient — they will not be updated by this example. If this happens consistently across all training examples, those weights are permanently frozen.

This is the **Dead ReLU problem**. It is not hypothetical: with aggressive learning rates or poor initialization, 10–40% of neurons can become permanently dead in the first few training steps. Leaky ReLU and GELU avoid this by ensuring a small non-zero gradient in the negative region.
