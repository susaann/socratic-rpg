## 5.6 What Makes Backpropagation Work: Three Properties

### Property 1: Modular Local Computation

Each node computes its local gradient independently, without knowing the global structure of the network. This modularity allows:

  * New activation functions and layer types to be added without changing the rest of the framework (just register the forward function and its derivative)
  * Parallel computation across independent subgraphs
  * Automatic verification of gradients via finite-difference checking



### Property 2: Efficient Reuse of Intermediate Computations

The key insight of backpropagation (Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams, 1986; also Werbos, 1974) was that the **same intermediate values** needed for the forward pass are exactly what is needed for the backward pass. Computing δ(l)\delta^{(l)}δ(l) requires h(l)\mathbf{h}^{(l)}h(l) (already computed in the forward pass), z(l)\mathbf{z}^{(l)}z(l) (needed for the activation derivative), and δ(l+1)\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l+1)}δ(l+1) (already computed in the backward pass). Nothing needs to be recomputed from scratch.

### Property 3: Exact Gradients at Zero Marginal Cost

Unlike finite-difference approximations of gradients (which require O(W)O(W)O(W) forward passes and introduce numerical error), backpropagation computes **exact** gradients (up to floating-point precision) in O(1)O(1)O(1) forward + backward passes. This exactness is critical for stable training — noisy gradient estimates introduce systematic bias that can prevent convergence.
