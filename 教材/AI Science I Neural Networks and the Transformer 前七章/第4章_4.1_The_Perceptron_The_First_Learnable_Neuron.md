## 4.1 The Perceptron: The First Learnable Neuron

### Rosenblatt's Model (1958)

Frank Rosenblatt at Cornell introduced the **perceptron** in 1958 ‚ÄĒ the first computational model that could _learn_ from data. Its design was inspired by neuroscience: biological neurons receive electrical signals from other neurons and "fire" when the total input exceeds a threshold.

The mathematical definition is minimal:

The XOR limitation is a specific instance of a general principle: a single layer can only represent **linear decision boundaries**. Any problem requiring a curved or multi-region boundary is beyond its reach.

The Minsky-Papert result contributed to a decade-long decline in neural network research funding ‚ÄĒ the "first AI winter" (approximately 1969‚Äď1982). The result was technically correct, but its implied conclusion ‚ÄĒ that neural networks were a dead end ‚ÄĒ proved wrong once multi-layer architectures and backpropagation were understood.

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection: Linear Separability in Geometry, Combinatorics, and Support Vector Machines**
> 
> **Convex geometry** characterizes linear separability precisely: two finite sets A,B‚äāRnA, B \subset \mathbb{R}^nA,B‚äāRn are linearly separable if and only if their convex hulls do not intersect (conv(A)‚ą©conv(B)=‚ąÖ\text{conv}(A) \cap \text{conv}(B) = \emptysetconv(A)‚ą©conv(B)=‚ąÖ). The separation hyperplane exists by the separating hyperplane theorem, a corollary of the Hahn-Banach theorem from functional analysis.
> 
> **Combinatorics** counts how often linear separability fails. The probability that mmm points drawn uniformly from Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn with random ¬Ī1\pm 1¬Ī1 labels are linearly separable is approximately 2‚ąím+n2^{-m+n}2‚ąím+n for m‚Čęnm \gg nm‚Čęn. This is Cover's theorem (1965): the proportion of linearly separable dichotomies of mmm points in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn decreases exponentially in m/nm/nm/n. Neural networks escape this by creating internal representations in high-dimensional space, where linear separability is much more likely.
> 
> **Support vector machines** (Vapnik, 1995) address linear separability directly: they find the maximum-margin separating hyperplane when data are linearly separable. Kernel SVMs handle nonlinear boundaries by implicitly mapping inputs to a high-dimensional feature space where linear separation is possible ‚ÄĒ the "kernel trick." This is a conceptually different solution to the XOR problem than neural networks: rather than stacking nonlinear layers, SVMs embed data into a high-dimensional space.
