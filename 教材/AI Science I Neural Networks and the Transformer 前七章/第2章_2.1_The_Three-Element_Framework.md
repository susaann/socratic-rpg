## 2.1 The Three-Element Framework

Every supervised learning problem, regardless of domain or architecture, is an instance of the same abstract structure. Before a single number is computed, you must specify three things:

  1. **Hypothesis space** H\mathcal{H}H: the family of functions your model is allowed to be. A linear model restricts hypotheses to {f:f(x)=w⊤x+b}\\{f : f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b\\}{f:f(x)=w⊤x+b}; a neural network with ReLU activations defines a much larger family of piecewise-linear functions.

  2. **Loss function** L\mathcal{L}L: a scalar that measures how badly a particular hypothesis f∈Hf \in \mathcal{H}f∈H performs on a training example (x,y)(x, y)(x,y). The loss function converts an ill-defined notion of "badness" into an optimization target.

  3. **Optimizer** : an algorithm that searches H\mathcal{H}H to find the hypothesis that minimizes the aggregate loss over the training set.




The framework is deliberately minimal. It says nothing about what H\mathcal{H}H looks like internally, nothing about whether the loss is convex, nothing about whether the data are i.i.d. These are choices that determine the character of a particular model. The framework itself is universal.

### Why Each Element Is Necessary

Remove the hypothesis space and you have no notion of what "a model" is — the optimizer has no space to search.

Remove the loss function and you have no signal — the optimizer cannot tell whether one hypothesis is better than another.

Remove the optimizer and the hypothesis space and loss function together define the problem but provide no procedure for solving it.

This tripartite structure also tells you where to intervene when a model fails:

  * **Underfitting** (the model cannot represent the true relationship) → expand the hypothesis space, or choose a richer function class
  * **Overfitting** (the model fits training noise) → constrain the hypothesis space, add regularization, or collect more data
  * **Optimization failure** (the model converges to a bad local minimum) → change the optimizer or its hyperparameters



The rest of this chapter makes the first two elements — hypothesis space and loss — mathematically precise. Optimization has its own chapter (Chapter 3).
