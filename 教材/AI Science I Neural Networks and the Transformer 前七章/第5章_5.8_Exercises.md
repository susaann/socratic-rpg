### Concept Check

**5.1** What are the two inputs a node needs to execute its backward pass? What does it compute from them?

Answer

A node needs:

  1. **Upstream gradient** ‚ąāL‚ąāz\frac{\partial L}{\partial z}‚ąāz‚ąāL‚Äč ‚ÄĒ the gradient of the loss with respect to the node's output zzz, arriving from the downstream (output-side) portion of the graph
  2. **Local gradient** ‚ąāz‚ąāxi\frac{\partial z}{\partial x_i}‚ąāxi‚Äč‚ąāz‚Äč ‚ÄĒ the partial derivative of the node's output with respect to each of its inputs, computed from the node's own operation (and any cached forward-pass values needed)



From these, the node computes the **downstream gradient** for each input xix_ixi‚Äč:

‚ąāL‚ąāxi=‚ąāL‚ąāz‚čÖ‚ąāz‚ąāxi\frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial x_i}‚ąāxi‚Äč‚ąāL‚Äč=‚ąāz‚ąāL‚Äč‚čÖ‚ąāxi‚Äč‚ąāz‚Äč

This is the gradient passed further upstream (toward the inputs/parameters of the network). The backward pass terminates when the leaf nodes (parameters) are reached ‚ÄĒ their gradients are exactly what the optimizer needs.

**5.5** Suppose you have a three-layer network and discover that all gradients for parameters in the first layer are near zero, but parameters in the third layer have large gradients. Using the backpropagation recursion őī(l)=[(W(l+1))‚ä§őī(l+1)]‚äôŌÉ‚Ä≤(l)(z(l))\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)} = [(\mathbf{W}^{(l+1)})^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l+1)}] \odot \sigma'^{(l)}(\mathbf{z}^{(l)})őī(l)=[(W(l+1))‚ä§őī(l+1)]‚äôŌÉ‚Ä≤(l)(z(l)), identify three possible causes.

**Cause 1: Vanishing activation derivative** ‚ÄĒ the activation function at layer 1 (or 2) is in saturation. **Cause 2: Dead ReLU neurons** ‚ÄĒ most neurons in layer 1 or 2 have z‚Č§0z \leq 0z‚Č§0, so ReLU‚Ä≤(z)=0\text{ReLU}'(z) = 0ReLU‚Ä≤(z)=0. **Cause 3: Weight matrix with small spectral norm** ‚ÄĒ if W(2)\mathbf{W}^{(2)}W(2) or W(3)\mathbf{W}^{(3)}W(3) has small singular values (e.g., due to poor initialization or excessive weight decay), the multiplication (W(l+1))‚ä§őī(l+1)(\mathbf{W}^{(l+1)})^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l+1)}(W(l+1))‚ä§őī(l+1) contracts the gradient magnitude. **Diagnostic** : plot the mean squared gradient norm per layer; a gradient norm that drops as you go deeper indicates one of these causes. ### Think Deeper

**5.7** Backpropagation was popularized by Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams (1986) but had been independently discovered multiple times before (Werbos, 1974; Linnainmaa, 1970). Why did it take until 1986 for backpropagation to have practical impact? What combination of algorithmic, hardware, and data changes enabled the deep learning revolution that started around 2012?

Answer

**Why backpropagation stalled before 1986** : The algorithm was known but several prerequisites were missing:

  1. **Insufficient data** : the datasets available before the internet era were too small to train networks that outperformed well-tuned hand-crafted features. A network that overfits 1,000 examples is useless.
  2. **Insufficient compute** : 1970s‚Äď1980s hardware could not train networks with more than a few thousand parameters in reasonable time. GPU acceleration was decades away.
  3. **The vanishing gradient problem** : sigmoid and tanh activations caused gradients to vanish in networks deeper than 3‚Äď4 layers, limiting expressivity.
  4. **No good initialization schemes** : Xavier initialization (2010) and He initialization (2015) were not known; random initialization often led to saturated activations from the first forward pass.



**What changed by 2012 (AlexNet)** :

  1. **Data** : ImageNet (2009) provided 1.2 million labeled images ‚ÄĒ enough to train a large network without overfitting and to test on a standard benchmark.
  2. **Compute** : NVIDIA GPU parallelism made training a deep network in days rather than years. The AlexNet paper was notable for implementing the forward and backward pass in CUDA.
  3. **ReLU activations** (Nair & Hinton, 2010): eliminated vanishing gradients in the positive half, enabling 8+ layer networks.
  4. **Dropout** (Hinton et al., 2012): effective regularization for large networks, preventing overfitting on the newly available large datasets.
  5. **Improved initialization and normalization** : better understanding of weight initialization scales.



The deep learning revolution was not triggered by a single algorithmic discovery but by the simultaneous convergence of data, compute, and a small number of architectural improvements that made training deep networks reliable.
