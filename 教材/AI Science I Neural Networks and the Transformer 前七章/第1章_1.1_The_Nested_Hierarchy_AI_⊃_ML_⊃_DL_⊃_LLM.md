## 1.1 The Nested Hierarchy: AI ⊃ ML ⊃ DL ⊃ LLM

The field of artificial intelligence is not a single discipline. It is a set of nested research programs, each inheriting and extending the methods of its predecessor:

AI⊃ML⊃DL⊃LLM\text{AI} \supset \text{ML} \supset \text{DL} \supset \text{LLM}AI⊃ML⊃DL⊃LLM

Understanding this nesting is not merely taxonomic. It has a practical implication: to understand why large language models work, you need to understand why the Transformer works; to understand the Transformer, you need to understand deep learning's optimization machinery; to understand that machinery, you need the basic framework of machine learning. Each layer has its own history of problems and solutions. This series works through that history in order, because every architecture introduced in later chapters is the answer to a question that an earlier architecture could not answer.

### 1.1.1 Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence, as a formal discipline, began at a summer workshop at Dartmouth College in the summer of 1956. John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon submitted a proposal containing a sentence that defined the field's ambitions for the next seven decades:

> "We propose that a 2 month, 10 man study of artificial intelligence be carried out during the summer of 1956... The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."

The conjecture — that intelligence is describable and therefore programmable — was both the founding assumption and the central tension of AI research. The symbolic AI program of the 1950s–80s took it literally: if intelligence is precise description, then build a system that stores precise descriptions (logical rules) and executes precise reasoning over them. The connectionist and statistical programs of the following decades challenged the literal interpretation: perhaps intelligence is not precisely describable as rules, but can still be learned from data.

A few historical figures are worth knowing precisely because their contributions span multiple paradigms:

**Alan Turing (1912–1954)** proposed, in his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," a behavioral test for machine intelligence — if a machine can converse indistinguishably from a human, call it intelligent. More importantly for this series, Turing proposed the "child machine" thought experiment: rather than programming an adult mind, program a child's mind with the capacity to learn. This is the first articulation of the idea that would become machine learning.

**Herbert Simon (1916–2001)** won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1978 and simultaneously built some of the first AI systems. With Allen Newell, he developed the Logic Theorist (1956) and the General Problem Solver (1959) — early programs that proved mathematical theorems and solved structured puzzles by searching a space of possible reasoning steps. Simon introduced the concept of _bounded rationality_ : agents with limited computational resources cannot optimize globally, so they adopt _satisficing_ strategies — searching until they find a solution that is good enough, then stopping.

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection**
> 
> _Biology / Ecology_ : Satisficing behavior appears throughout animal foraging theory. The optimal foraging model predicts that animals should maximize energy intake per unit time; empirical studies find that most animals instead use threshold-based rules ("stop searching when prey density drops below xxx"), which are computationally cheaper and nearly as effective in typical environments. The mathematics is the same as Simon's: an explicit optimum is replaced by a good-enough stopping criterion.
> 
> _Computer Science / Search Algorithms_ : Beam search — the algorithm used by language models to generate text — is a direct implementation of Simon's satisficing: instead of searching all possible continuations (exponentially expensive), maintain only the kkk highest-scoring partial sequences at each step. When k=1k = 1k=1, this is greedy search; when k→∞k \to \inftyk→∞, it converges to exact search. The beam width kkk is a computational budget parameter.
> 
> _Statistical Physics_ : Simulated annealing, a general-purpose optimization algorithm, formalizes the satisficing/optimizing tradeoff using a temperature parameter. At high temperature, the algorithm accepts worse solutions with positive probability (exploration); as temperature decreases, it becomes increasingly greedy (satisficing). The "cooling schedule" governs the transition from global search to local exploitation.

### 1.1.2 Machine Learning

Machine learning is the subfield of AI that studies algorithms which improve at a task through experience — that is, by processing data. Tom Mitchell's 1997 definition remains the cleanest formalization:

> A computer program is said to _learn_ from experience EEE with respect to task TTT and performance measure PPP, if its performance at TTT, as measured by PPP, improves with experience EEE.

Translated into the notation this series will use throughout: given a dataset {(xi,yi)}i=1n\\{(x_i, y_i)\\}_{i=1}^n{(xi​,yi​)}i=1n​ (experience EEE), a learning algorithm outputs a function f^:X→Y\hat{f}: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}f^​:X→Y (performing task TTT) such that some loss measure L\mathcal{L}L (performance PPP) is minimized on new, unseen data.

Machine learning comprises three major learning paradigms:

**Supervised learning** : the training data consists of input-output pairs (xi,yi)(x_i, y_i)(xi​,yi​). The algorithm learns a mapping from inputs to outputs. Examples: predicting a continuous value (regression), predicting a category (classification). This is the setting that Chapters 2–9 focus on.

**Unsupervised learning** : only inputs {xi}\\{x_i\\}{xi​} are available, with no labels. The algorithm discovers structure in the data: clusters, low-dimensional representations, probability distributions. Word embeddings (Part IV of this volume) are a form of unsupervised learning.

**Reinforcement learning** : an agent interacts with an environment, receives reward signals, and learns a policy that maximizes cumulative reward. The agent learns not from a fixed dataset but from its own experience of acting in the world. Reinforcement learning is the subject of Volume II (in the context of aligning language models to human preferences).

### 1.1.3 Deep Learning

Deep learning is the subfield of machine learning that uses neural networks with many layers. The word "deep" refers to the number of layers between input and output. A "shallow" network with one or two hidden layers can already approximate arbitrary functions in principle (Chapter 4 will prove this). What depth buys, beyond the theoretical guarantee, is the ability to learn _hierarchical representations_ automatically: low layers learn simple features, higher layers combine them into progressively more abstract concepts.

Three conditions converged around 2012 to make deep learning practical at scale:

**Large datasets** : the ImageNet dataset, compiled by Fei-Fei Li and collaborators starting in 2007, contained 14 million labeled images across 1000 categories. Networks with millions of parameters require millions of training examples to avoid overfitting — the phenomenon in which a model memorizes training data without generalizing.

**GPU-accelerated computation** : the core operation in training a neural network is multiplying large matrices. Modern graphics processing units (GPUs), designed for parallel rendering of game graphics, turned out to be extremely efficient at exactly this operation — far more so than general-purpose CPUs, because they contain thousands of simple arithmetic units that execute simultaneously.

**Algorithmic innovations** : techniques that had been known theoretically but had not been combined effectively — the ReLU activation function (Chapter 4), dropout regularization (Chapter 6), improved weight initialization (Chapter 7) — were assembled into architectures that actually trained without the gradient pathologies that had plagued deep networks for decades.

The watershed event was AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton, 2012), which reduced the top-5 error rate on ImageNet from 26% (the previous best) to 15% — a margin so large that it was immediately clear a new era had begun.

### 1.1.4 Large Language Models

Large language models are deep learning systems trained on text. They are "large" in two senses: in the number of parameters (ranging from hundreds of millions to trillions) and in the volume of training data (hundreds of billions to trillions of tokens). They are "language models" in the technical sense: they assign probability distributions over sequences of tokens, and can generate text by sampling from those distributions.

The defining architecture is the Transformer, introduced in 2017 and the subject of Part VI of this volume. What makes LLMs remarkable is not just their scale but their versatility: a single model trained to predict the next word in text turns out to develop representations that transfer to virtually any language task — translation, summarization, question answering, reasoning, code generation — without task-specific design.

### 1.1.5 Why the Hierarchy Matters

The nesting AI ⊃ ML ⊃ DL ⊃ LLM is not just a taxonomy — it is an inheritance structure. Every layer uses the tools of the layer below:

  * To understand a large language model, you need to understand the Transformer's self-attention mechanism (Part VI).
  * To understand self-attention, you need to understand how neural networks are trained — loss functions, gradient descent, backpropagation (Parts I–II).
  * To understand training, you need the basic machine learning framework — what we are optimizing, and why (this chapter and Chapter 2).



This series works through this inheritance from the bottom up. You are not starting from scratch; you are entering at the mathematical foundation — linear algebra, calculus, probability — and building upward.
