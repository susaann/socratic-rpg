### Prediction Versus Inference

A distinction that often goes unmarked in machine learning courses: **prediction** and **statistical inference** are not the same goal.

_Prediction_ asks: given a new input xxx, what value of yyy should I guess to minimize expected loss? The answer is a function f(x)f(x)f(x) — a mapping from inputs to outputs.

_Statistical inference_ asks: what is the causal or structural relationship between xxx and yyy? The answer is a model with interpretable parameters, confidence intervals, and assumptions about the data-generating process.

A model trained by ERM can achieve excellent prediction accuracy with parameters that carry no causal interpretation. A linear regression on confounded data can have a low MSE while estimating a coefficient that is wildly different from the true causal effect. Machine learning is predominantly a _prediction_ technology; extracting causal conclusions requires additional structure (randomization, instrumental variables, difference-in-differences).

This is not a criticism of machine learning — it is a clarification of what it promises. The methods in this book are prediction methods. When prediction is the goal, ERM is a principled and powerful approach.
