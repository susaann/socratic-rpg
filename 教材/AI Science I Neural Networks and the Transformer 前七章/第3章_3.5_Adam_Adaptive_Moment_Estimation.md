## 3.5 Adam: Adaptive Moment Estimation

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation), introduced by Kingma & Ba (2015), is the most widely used optimizer in deep learning. It combines momentum (first-moment estimation) with per-parameter adaptive learning rates (second-moment estimation) and includes a principled bias correction for the initialization artifact.

### The Full Derivation

**Initialize** : m0=0\mathbf{m}_0 = \mathbf{0}m0​=0, v0=0\mathbf{v}_0 = \mathbf{0}v0​=0.

At each step t=1,2,3,…t = 1, 2, 3, \ldotst=1,2,3,…:

**Step 1 — Compute gradient** :

gt=∇wL(wt)\mathbf{g}_t = \nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_t)gt​=∇w​L(wt​)

In the mini-batch setting, gt\mathbf{g}_tgt​ is a noisy estimate of the true gradient.

**Step 2 — Update first moment (momentum term)** :

mt=β1mt−1+(1−β1)gt\mathbf{m}_t = \beta_1 \mathbf{m}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1) \mathbf{g}_tmt​=β1​mt−1​+(1−β1​)gt​

This is the EWMA of the gradient, estimating the **first moment** (mean direction) of the gradient. Unrolling:

mt=(1−β1)∑k=1tβ1t−kgk\mathbf{m}_t = (1-\beta_1)\sum_{k=1}^t \beta_1^{t-k} \mathbf{g}_kmt​=(1−β1​)k=1∑t​β1t−k​gk​

With default β1=0.9\beta_1 = 0.9β1​=0.9, the half-life is approximately 10 steps. mt\mathbf{m}_tmt​ is the smoothed gradient direction.

**Step 3 — Update second moment** :

vt=β2vt−1+(1−β2)gt2\mathbf{v}_t = \beta_2 \mathbf{v}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2) \mathbf{g}_t^2vt​=β2​vt−1​+(1−β2​)gt2​

This is the EWMA of the **element-wise squared** gradient, estimating the second moment E[g2]\mathbb{E}[g^2]E[g2] of each coordinate. With default β2=0.999\beta_2 = 0.999β2​=0.999, the half-life is approximately 1000 steps — the second moment estimate changes slowly, giving a stable measure of each parameter's gradient magnitude.

**Step 4 — Bias correction** :

Both mt\mathbf{m}_tmt​ and vt\mathbf{v}_tvt​ are initialized at zero. In early training, this zero initialization creates a systematic underestimate. We now derive the correction precisely.

Taking the expectation of mt\mathbf{m}_tmt​ under the stationarity assumption E[gk]=E[g]\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}_k] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]E[gk​]=E[g]:

E[mt]=(1−β1)∑k=1tβ1t−k⋅E[g]\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{m}_t] = (1-\beta_1)\sum_{k=1}^t \beta_1^{t-k} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]E[mt​]=(1−β1​)k=1∑t​β1t−k​⋅E[g] =(1−β1)E[g]∑j=0t−1β1j=(1−β1)E[g]⋅1−β1t1−β1=(1−β1t)E[g]= (1-\beta_1) \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}] \sum_{j=0}^{t-1} \beta_1^j = (1-\beta_1) \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}] \cdot \frac{1-\beta_1^t}{1-\beta_1} = (1-\beta_1^t)\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]=(1−β1​)E[g]j=0∑t−1​β1j​=(1−β1​)E[g]⋅1−β1​1−β1t​​=(1−β1t​)E[g]

The factor (1−β1t)(1-\beta_1^t)(1−β1t​) is the bias. At step t=1t=1t=1 with β1=0.9\beta_1 = 0.9β1​=0.9: this factor is 0.10.10.1, meaning m1\mathbf{m}_1m1​ is only 10% of the true gradient mean. At step t=10t=10t=10: 1−0.910≈0.651 - 0.9^{10} \approx 0.651−0.910≈0.65. At step t=100t=100t=100: 1−0.9100≈1.01 - 0.9^{100} \approx 1.01−0.9100≈1.0 — the bias has vanished.

**Bias-corrected estimates** :

m^t=mt1−β1t,v^t=vt1−β2t\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t = \frac{\mathbf{m}_t}{1-\beta_1^t}, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{v}}_t = \frac{\mathbf{v}_t}{1-\beta_2^t}m^t​=1−β1t​mt​​,v^t​=1−β2t​vt​​

Verification: E[m^t]=(1−β1t)E[g]1−β1t=E[g]\mathbb{E}[\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t] = \frac{(1-\beta_1^t)\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]}{1-\beta_1^t} = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]E[m^t​]=1−β1t​(1−β1t​)E[g]​=E[g] — unbiased. The same argument applies to v^t\hat{\mathbf{v}}_tv^t​.

**Numerical example** at t=1t = 1t=1: m^1=m1/0.1=10m1\hat{\mathbf{m}}_1 = \mathbf{m}_1 / 0.1 = 10 \mathbf{m}_1m^1​=m1​/0.1=10m1​, and v^1=v1/0.001=1000v1\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1 = \mathbf{v}_1 / 0.001 = 1000 \mathbf{v}_1v^1​=v1​/0.001=1000v1​ — the correction is dramatic in the first step.

**Step 5 — Parameter update** :

wt+1=wt−ηm^tv^t+ϵ\mathbf{w}_{t+1} = \mathbf{w}_t - \eta \frac{\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t} + \epsilon}wt+1​=wt​−ηv^t​​+ϵm^t​​

where ϵ=10−8\epsilon = 10^{-8}ϵ=10−8 prevents division by zero. Element-wise for parameter jjj:

wt+1,j=wt,j−η⋅m^t,jv^t,j+ϵw_{t+1,j} = w_{t,j} - \eta \cdot \frac{\hat{m}_{t,j}}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_{t,j}} + \epsilon}wt+1,j​=wt,j​−η⋅v^t,j​​+ϵm^t,j​​

The **complete Adam algorithm** :

gt=∇wL(wt)mt=β1mt−1+(1−β1)gtvt=β2vt−1+(1−β2)gt2m^t=mt/(1−β1t)v^t=vt/(1−β2t)wt+1=wt−η m^t/(v^t+ϵ)\boxed{\begin{aligned} \mathbf{g}_t &= \nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_t) \\\ \mathbf{m}_t &= \beta_1 \mathbf{m}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1) \mathbf{g}_t \\\ \mathbf{v}_t &= \beta_2 \mathbf{v}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2) \mathbf{g}_t^2 \\\ \hat{\mathbf{m}}_t &= \mathbf{m}_t / (1-\beta_1^t) \\\ \hat{\mathbf{v}}_t &= \mathbf{v}_t / (1-\beta_2^t) \\\ \mathbf{w}_{t+1} &= \mathbf{w}_t - \eta\, \hat{\mathbf{m}}_t / (\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t} + \epsilon) \end{aligned}}gt​mt​vt​m^t​v^t​wt+1​​=∇w​L(wt​)=β1​mt−1​+(1−β1​)gt​=β2​vt−1​+(1−β2​)gt2​=mt​/(1−β1t​)=vt​/(1−β2t​)=wt​−ηm^t​/(v^t​​+ϵ)​​

**Default hyperparameters** : η=10−3\eta = 10^{-3}η=10−3, β1=0.9\beta_1 = 0.9β1​=0.9, β2=0.999\beta_2 = 0.999β2​=0.999, ϵ=10−8\epsilon = 10^{-8}ϵ=10−8.

### What Each Component Contributes

The update Δwj=−η⋅m^t,j/(v^t,j+ϵ)\Delta w_j = -\eta \cdot \hat{m}_{t,j} / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_{t,j}} + \epsilon)Δwj​=−η⋅m^t,j​/(v^t,j​​+ϵ) has a clear decomposition:

  * **Numerator m^t,j\hat{m}_{t,j}m^t,j​**: smoothed gradient direction — provides the signal of which way to step, with noise averaged out by momentum
  * **Denominator v^t,j\sqrt{\hat{v}_{t,j}}v^t,j​​**: gradient magnitude history — the **adaptive learning rate** that scales each parameter's step size by its past gradient magnitude



This means every parameter gets its own effective learning rate ηjeff=η/(v^t,j+ϵ)\eta_j^\text{eff} = \eta / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_{t,j}} + \epsilon)ηjeff​=η/(v^t,j​​+ϵ):

  * Parameters with consistently large gradients (large v^t,j\hat{v}_{t,j}v^t,j​) get smaller steps — preventing overshooting
  * Parameters with consistently small gradients (small v^t,j\hat{v}_{t,j}v^t,j​) get larger steps — accelerating their convergence



Adam = Momentum + RMSProp + Bias Correction.

### AdamW: Decoupled Weight Decay

Loshchilov & Hutter (2019) identified a subtle issue: adding L2 regularization by including λw\lambda \mathbf{w}λw in the gradient before Adam's update is not equivalent to weight decay, because the adaptive scaling in the denominator distorts the regularization effect. **AdamW** applies weight decay separately:

wt+1=(1−ηλ)wt−ηm^tv^t+ϵ\mathbf{w}_{t+1} = (1 - \eta\lambda)\mathbf{w}_t - \eta \frac{\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t} + \epsilon}wt+1​=(1−ηλ)wt​−ηv^t​​+ϵm^t​​

The weight decay operates directly on parameters before the gradient update, maintaining the intended regularization semantics. AdamW is the standard optimizer for training Transformers and large language models.

> **Cross-Disciplinary Connection: Adaptive Methods in Control Theory, Statistics, and Signal Processing**
> 
> **Optimal control theory** has used adaptive gain scheduling for decades. A PID controller adjusts its proportional, integral, and derivative gains based on the observed error signal. The integral term in PID — accumulating past errors — is precisely the momentum term in Adam's numerator; the derivative term (scaling by recent error change rate) resembles the denominator's gradient-magnitude scaling.
> 
> **Statistics** developed adaptive estimation in the form of the Kalman filter (1960), which maintains a running estimate of the true state and scales the update step by the Kalman gain — a ratio of predicted uncertainty to observed uncertainty. Adam's m^t/v^t\hat{m}_t / \sqrt{\hat{v}_t}m^t​/v^t​​ has the same structure: a signal estimate divided by a noise estimate. The Kalman filter is Adam's Bayesian generalization.
> 
> **Natural gradient methods** (Amari, 1998) provide the theoretically principled adaptive method: scale the gradient by the inverse Fisher information matrix F−1F^{-1}F−1 to account for the geometry of the parameter space. Adam approximates this with a diagonal F−1F^{-1}F−1 (using only per-coordinate gradient variance rather than the full covariance). K-FAC and other second-order methods use richer approximations, at higher computational cost.
