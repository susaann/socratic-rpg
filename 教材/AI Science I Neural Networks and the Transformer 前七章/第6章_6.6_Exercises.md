**6.2** Explain why the softmax gradient ∂L/∂zj=pj−yj\partial L / \partial z_j = p_j - y_j∂L/∂zj​=pj​−yj​ does not suffer from the vanishing gradient problem that plagues sigmoid in hidden layers.

For sigmoid in hidden layers, the gradient must pass through the activation function derivative σ′(z)=σ(z)(1−σ(z))≤0.25\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z)) \leq 0.25σ′(z)=σ(z)(1−σ(z))≤0.25. This derivative is small when the neuron saturates, causing vanishing gradients.

For the output layer's softmax + cross-entropy, the gradient pj−yjp_j - y_jpj​−yj​ is the _combined_ derivative of loss through softmax, not the derivative of softmax alone. This combined gradient does not vanish: for the true class, pc−1p_c - 1pc​−1 is close to −1-1−1 when the model is confidently wrong (which is early in training, when the gradient signal should be strongest). The gradient is bounded away from zero until the model is actually confident _and_ correct.

This is a key advantage of using the natural loss function for each output type: maximum likelihood estimators produce gradients that are informative exactly when the model needs them most.

**6.4** Why is zero initialization of biases safe, while zero initialization of weights is catastrophic?

Answer

The symmetry problem with zero weight initialization: all neurons in a layer share identical weight vectors, receive identical inputs, compute identical pre-activations, and receive identical gradients. The symmetry is preserved through every gradient update — all neurons remain identical forever, regardless of training. The network functions as width-1 at every hidden layer.

Biases do not mediate this symmetry. Each neuron's bias is a scalar offset that shifts its pre-activation. Even with all biases at zero, if the weight vectors are different (random), neurons compute different pre-activations and different gradients. After one gradient step, biases also differ. The bias's job is not to break inter-neuron symmetry — it is to shift the activation threshold of an individual neuron, which it does correctly whether initialized at zero or not.

### Application Problems

**6.5** You are designing a network to predict daily stock returns (a continuous value that can be positive or negative, with no natural upper bound). What output activation should you use and why? What loss function? Is there any preprocessing of the target variable you would recommend?

Answer

**Output activation** : linear (identity). Stock returns are real-valued and unbounded. Sigmoid would restrict output to (0,1)(0,1)(0,1); softmax requires discrete classes. For any regression task, the output layer should be linear.

**Loss function** : technically MSE if you believe returns have Gaussian noise. However, financial returns are heavy-tailed (occasional extreme moves). MAE or Huber loss are more robust alternatives — they correspond to Laplace and "clipped Gaussian" noise models, which better match the actual distribution.

**Preprocessing recommendations** :

  1. **Normalization** : standardize the target to have zero mean and unit variance over the training set. This makes the loss surface more uniform and prevents the optimizer from spending most effort fitting the large-return outliers. Invert the standardization at inference time.
  2. **Outlier clipping** : clip targets at ±3 standard deviations, especially if using MSE. A single extreme event with residual 100 contributes 10,00010,00010,000 to MSE — 10,000× the contribution of a typical residual of 1.
  3. Consider predicting log returns rather than raw returns to stabilize variance and make the approximately additive structure of compounding explicit.

### Think Deeper

**6.6** The text argues that Xavier and He initialization solve the variance explosion/vanishing problem "for all depths." But in practice, extremely deep networks (1000+ layers) still have training difficulties even with correct initialization. Why? What additional mechanism is needed, and what does it do?

Answer

Xavier/He initialization preserves variance at initialization (t=0t = 0t=0), but cannot guarantee that variance remains stable as training proceeds. After even one gradient step, the weights change, and the carefully calibrated initialization conditions no longer hold.

For very deep networks, two additional failure modes emerge:

**1\. Variance drift during training** : as weights update, the distribution of pre-activations shifts away from the initialized distribution. In deep networks, small drifts in each layer multiply over LLL layers into a large drift at the output, destabilizing the loss landscape.

**2\. Internal covariate shift** : the distribution of inputs to each layer changes as earlier layers update, requiring each layer to continually adapt to a moving target distribution — slowing learning and requiring careful learning rate selection.

The additional mechanism needed is **Batch Normalization** (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015, Chapter 7). BatchNorm normalizes the pre-activations of each layer to have zero mean and unit variance within each mini-batch, then applies a learnable rescaling (γ\gammaγ) and shift (β\betaβ). This:

  * Re-establishes the variance-stability condition after each parameter update
  * Allows higher learning rates (the loss surface is better conditioned)
  * Provides a small amount of regularization through the mini-batch noise in the mean/variance estimates
  * Makes the network dramatically less sensitive to initialization quality



With BatchNorm, networks with hundreds of layers train reliably. Residual connections (Chapter 9) address a different but related issue — the gradient transmission problem — and together BatchNorm + residual connections are what make 100–1000 layer networks trainable.
