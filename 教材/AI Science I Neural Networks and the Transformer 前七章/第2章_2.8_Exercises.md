### Application Problems

**2.5** You are fitting a model to predict housing prices. You observe that your training MSE is 0.01 but your test MSE is 0.35. Using the bias-variance framework, diagnose the problem and propose two interventions.

Answer

**Diagnosis** : The large gap between training MSE (0.01) and test MSE (0.35) is the signature of **high variance / overfitting**. The model has fit the training data very closely (low empirical risk) but does not generalize (high expected risk). The bias term is likely small (the model can represent the true function) but the variance term is large (the learned function is highly sensitive to the training set).

**Intervention 1: Add regularization** ‚Äî Introduce L2 (weight decay) or L1 regularization to constrain the parameter magnitudes. This increases the effective prior toward smaller parameters and reduces variance at the cost of a small increase in bias. For neural networks, dropout is a form of implicit regularization that also reduces variance.

**Intervention 2: Collect more training data** ‚Äî More data reduces variance directly: with more examples, the learned function varies less across training sets (as argued in Exercise 2.2). If data collection is cheap, this is often the highest-leverage intervention.

**Intervention 3 (if data is fixed)** : Reduce model complexity ‚Äî use a smaller network, fewer layers, or a less expressive architecture. This constrains the hypothesis space and reduces variance at the cost of bias.

**2.8** The "no free lunch" theorem (Wolpert, 1996) states that averaged over all possible data-generating distributions, every learning algorithm has the same expected generalization error. How does this theorem coexist with the obvious practical success of deep neural networks on image and language tasks? What assumption does the theorem make that does not hold in practice?

Answer

**The theorem's statement** : The no-free-lunch (NFL) theorem averages performance over the _uniform distribution over all possible target functions_. In this setting, any learning algorithm (including the one that ignores the data and returns a random function) performs as well as any other in expectation.

**The key assumption** : The uniform distribution over functions is the critical assumption. It treats a function mapping an image of a cat to "cat" as equally likely as a function mapping that image to "carburettor" or "œÄ\piœÄ." In other words, it assigns equal probability to functions that exhibit smooth, compositional, locally structured patterns and functions that are completely random.

**Why it does not hold in practice** : The real world is not a uniform sample from the space of all possible functions. Images of cats do cluster together in pixel space; words that appear in similar contexts do have similar meanings; physical systems do satisfy conservation laws. The functions we actually care about share structure: locality, hierarchy, compositionality, translation equivariance. Neural network architectures encode strong inductive biases toward exactly these structures (local filters, hierarchical depth, attention over paired elements).

**The practical coexistence** : Deep neural networks succeed because their inductive biases ‚Äî particularly spatial hierarchy in CNNs and sequence structure in transformers ‚Äî match the structure of natural data. The NFL theorem is a warning about algorithms that make no inductive assumptions; deep learning is successful precisely because it makes the _right_ inductive assumptions for the domain. Choosing the right hypothesis space is not "free" ‚Äî it requires domain knowledge. The work of the last decade has been figuring out which biases are right for which problems.
