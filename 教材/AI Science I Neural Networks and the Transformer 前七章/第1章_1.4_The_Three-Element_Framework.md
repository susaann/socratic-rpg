## 1.4 The Three-Element Framework

The most important abstract pattern in this volume is this: every machine learning algorithm — every algorithm, without exception — can be described by three elements.

**Element 1: The hypothesis space** H\mathcal{H}H. The set of functions the algorithm is willing to consider as candidate solutions. A linear regression model searches over all functions of the form f(x)=wTx+bf(x) = w^T x + bf(x)=wTx+b — the hypothesis space is the set of linear functions. A two-layer neural network with 100 hidden units searches over a much larger (and more complex) space of functions. The choice of hypothesis space encodes prior beliefs about the structure of the relationship to be learned.

**Element 2: The loss function** L\mathcal{L}L. A measure of how wrong a candidate hypothesis is on the training data. For regression, the mean squared error: L(f)=1n∑i=1n(f(xi)−yi)2\mathcal{L}(f) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - y_i)^2L(f)=n1​∑i=1n​(f(xi​)−yi​)2. For classification, the cross-entropy loss. The choice of loss function encodes what "being wrong" means for the specific problem — and as Chapter 2 will show, it often has a probabilistic interpretation as maximum likelihood estimation under a specific noise model.

**Element 3: The optimization algorithm**. The procedure for searching the hypothesis space to minimize the loss. For convex problems with a small hypothesis space, exact minimization is possible. For the large, non-convex hypothesis spaces of deep networks, gradient descent and its variants (Chapter 3) are the universal tool.

This three-element decomposition is not merely descriptive — it is generative. When you encounter a new learning algorithm, ask: what is its hypothesis space? What is its loss function? How does it optimize? The answers will tell you when the algorithm is likely to work and when it is likely to fail.

Every architecture in this series is a choice of hypothesis space. Linear regression chooses linear functions. Multilayer perceptrons choose smooth compositions of affine and nonlinear functions. CNNs choose functions with local, translation-equivariant structure. RNNs choose functions that update a state sequentially. Transformers choose functions that compute weighted averages of value representations, with query-key compatibility determining the weights. The "Transformer looks the way it does" question is, at the architectural level, a question about what hypothesis space is right for modeling language — and the answer requires understanding why every alternative hypothesis space proved inadequate.
