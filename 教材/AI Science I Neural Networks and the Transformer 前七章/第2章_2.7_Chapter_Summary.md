## 2.7 Chapter Summary

The three-element framework — hypothesis space, loss function, optimizer — is the common language of all supervised learning. The hypothesis space defines what functions are possible; the loss function defines what "good" means; the optimizer finds the best function.

The bias-variance decomposition reveals a fundamental tension: more expressive hypothesis spaces reduce bias but increase variance. Regularization controls the tradeoff by constraining the hypothesis space, and Bayesian inference reveals that every regularizer is a prior probability over parameters.

Loss functions are probabilistic models in disguise. MSE assumes Gaussian noise; cross-entropy assumes a categorical model. Choosing a loss function is choosing a noise model.

The framework is not peculiar to machine learning. Physicists, biologists, linguists, and engineers all specify hypothesis spaces (models), loss functions (objective criteria), and optimizers (fitting procedures) when they build predictive models. What changed in the deep learning era is the size of the hypothesis space and the computational machinery that makes searching it tractable.
